Conflict Resolution

“Peace is not the absence of conflict, but the ability to cope with it.”
— – Mahatma Gandhi

Attitudes and behaviors of teams that practice constructive conflict resolution

Solve the problem immediately:

Seek to resolve the conflict instead of letting it linger.

Increase communication

Both parties in disagreement get to express their opinions and viewpoints clearly.

Are empathic

Have the ability to ensure that a fair resolution takes into account both sides of the dispute.

Address underlying issues

  • Do not look for a quick fix and address the root of the problem.

  • Show true thoughts and concerns

  • Do not keep secrets and hold grudges.

Establish common ground

Share similar value and work toward a common purpose.

Differentiate different types of conflict

Process conflict

: Differing work styles among member

Relationship conflict

: Team members clash because of different personalities, attitudes, and values.

Status conflict:

Disagreements related to power and decision making rules

Know how to address the conflict:

  • Team members take a moment to regroup before attempting problem-solving.

  • Individual team members involved in the conflict talk about the disagreement and listen to each other carefully.

  • Discuss conflict as a group.

  • Discuss how to solve the conflict and seek input of all members.

  • Draw up a plan

  • Assign responsibilities to members and take them seriously

  • Brainstorm solutions

  • Choose a solution

  • Work on the solution

  • Follow up

  • Evaluate if the plan works by having team members respond to anonymous surveys.

Steps to follow to foster constructive conflict management in teams

Emphasize Process and not Product by …

Providing Directions

  • Clear definition of the problem to be solved with list of relevant variables

  • Necessary steps to solve the problem at hand

  • Assigning relevant tasks to different team members

  • Establishing communication style (email, zoom meetings, etc.) between group members.

  • Clarifying miles stones such as date of progress reports, final for ports, and presentation

Grand Rules

  • Have team members establish ground rules

  • Emphasize Respecting ground rules for effective function

  • Have teams role play ground rules

  • Agree on time required to do different parts

  • Set deadlines

  • Encourage team members to assess their strengths and weaknesses by taking anonymous surveys on constructs related to successful team building

  • Have members engage in group discussions to find out how traits such as procrastination, communication skills, empathy, and openness to criticism could potentially affect their group dynamics.

Monitoring Team Progress

  • Conduct weekly meetings with teams

  • Have teams share progress and potential challenges

  • Redirect team activities in the right direction

  • Members receive advice if necessary

Evaluating Team Progress

  • Conduct exit surveys and seek input of teams on how things went

  • Interview team members regarding team success

  • Seek client input regarding success of project

Overlap of the seven constructs imperative to successful team building

From the above discussion, one might conclude that we are repeating some of the seven constructs such as communication skills, empathy, and flexibility. It has to be elaborated that these constructs overlap and conflict resolution is one of the instances in which one needs to employ these constructs simultaneously. We created some arbitrary boundaries between the seven constructs of team building to define, clarify, and elaborate them. However, as we have concluded so far behaviors related to successful team building overlap and involve the simultaneous implementation of multiple constructs at the same time.

Example of a case study involving conflict

In a statistical consulting class, a team of seniors decided to work on a problem related to the analysis of “President Trump’s tweets and”calling covid19” the “Chinese virus” on the Chinese community. About a week after the project, multiple emails were received from members asking to split the team or join other teams.

In order to learn more about the dynamics of the group, the following steps were taken…

  • Had a half hour zoom meeting with each member.

  • Listened carefully to what each member had to say and tried to understand the problem from the perspective of each individual.

  • Met with the whole team as a group over zoom and let all the members speak about the dilemma they were facing.

  • It was interesting to notice that in meeting with all the team, all team members agreed verbally that they would be happy to keep the team together. However, their body language, tone of voice, and the comfort level they exhibited during their interaction on zoom was not aligned with what they said.

  • In spite of long meetings with individual members and meeting with the whole team, coming up with a solution to keep the team together and continue working on the same project was not successful.

The individual and group discussions showed that…

  • Half of the group members were “very excited” about this project and the other half did not seem to share the same level of enthusiasm.

  • The enthusiastic group took the project and ran with it. They created mathematical models for the analysis of the tweets as well as using NLP (Natural Language Processing) for analysis of open-ended comments.

  • The enthusiastic members were under the impression that the other members did not contributing to the project and that they were doing all of the work.

  • In spite of attending the meetings, the less enthusiastic members were not following what the other members were doing and did not feel like an integral part of the team.

Based on the information collected as well as the consent of the team, the members agreed to split the team into two groups and have each group work on a different project. Splitting the team may not look like the best way of resolving the conflict in question because we aspire to have the team members resolve their disagreement and continue to work together rather than split.

However, in January 2020 the COVID epidemic had just started and all instruction suddenly became online. Consequently, rather than working with a real client, teams used publicly available COVID data published by institutions such as John Hopkins and other online resources, and did extensive work on the effect of COVID on economic, social, and psychological issues.

(added) Most of the time, open communication, collaboration and respect go a long way towards solving conflict. Types of conflict can be categorized as:

1. Task Conflict happens when group members attempt to divide the work among themselves. But its basis 2. Relationship Conflict arise from variation in personality and all of its outward manifestations 3. Value Conflict arise from fundamental differences in personal values arising from political views and religious origins.

We have worked on task conflict through brainstorming ideas, collaborative problem solving and developing solutions as a team as opposed to assigning tasks to particular members by the instructor. Through this strategy team members are more likely to respect the proposed solution, complete the project successfully and have positive relations with their teammates.

Relationship conflict can be diffused through interpersonal discussion lead by the instructor bringing up the source of tension with a focus of listening to each person and demonstrating empathy and interest in their perspectives. If the conflict persists, it may help to enlist another instructor to come and join the discussion and offer input with empathy.

Value Conflict – suppose a team will be analyzing language data extracted from social media responses. One part of the team sees it as a purely quantitative modeling exercise while the other is interested in analyzing the underlying conceptual meaning (qualitative). To diffuse conflict, strive to remove "values" from the discussion while maintaining focus on the problem, that is, can teammates talk about an analytic solution that satisfies the entire team while removing personal values from the discussion?

Individual Exercises

: Write a fictitious case study involving using statistics to solve a real world problem. Suppose that a team of four people are working on this project. Answer the following questions:

  • Identify a real world problem that needs to be solved by using statistics/data science.

  • Write the problem to be solved clearly and within context.

  • Discuss a fictitious conflict that has happened between team members. The conflict can either be a process, a relationship, or a status conflict.

  • Discuss potential factors that could underlie this conflict. For example such factors could be lack of ground rules, emotional intelligence, skills, etc.

  • Elaborate why the factors you identified in part c are the underlying factor for the identified conflict.

  • What steps do you suggest the team members take to resolve this conflict?

  • What is the long-term effect of this experience on your conflict management skills?

Group Exercises

  • Get together with your team members and discuss the fictitious case you wrote in exercise one.

  • Discuss with your team members what you learned from this exercise.

  • With your team choose one of the case studies written.

  • Summarize in bullet points how you would approached solving this conflict before reading about conflict resolution and doing exercise one and after.
