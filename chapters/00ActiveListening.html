Active Listening

“Know how to listen, and you will profit even from those who talk badly.” — Plutarch

A generation ago a book titled “All I Really Need to Know I Learned in Kinder- garten” topped the bestseller list[@fulghum1986all]. For most of us, listening to others was taught when we entered our first classroom. If we were too chatty or restless we were told to quiet ourselves. We were told to “pay attention.” In schools, listening is generally “taught” by “osmosis” even though it accounts for a plurality (just under 50%) of our language skills[@oxford1993research]. In addition, this vital skill is relatively poorly understood as compared with speaking, reading, and writing. There are many reasons for this neglect[@osada2004listening] we might distill them into three:

* Conversing with another person takes place in a social context and in real time.
* Sound presents a myriad of possibilities, print, for example, has become increas-

ingly uniform. * The spoken word and the written word differ greatly in formality thus listening requires greater mental agility

We approach listening in the context of team building in a statistical consulting en- vironment through a mindfulness approach. With an emphasis on paying full attention to teammates and clients as opposed to hurrying or multitasking.

We will begin with a discussion of building the scaffolding for mindful listening.

Mindful Listening

Mindfulness is awareness.

As an example, when dieting, one strategy is “mindful eating” where the dieter is instructed to look beyond eating as simply inserting food in the one’s mouth and instead to be aware of other factors associated with eating. Thirst is such a factor. Thirst can be mistaken for hunger and without thinking, a person might eat. Mindful eating would inform the dieter “to drink a cup or two of water” and then “wait” before making the decision to eat.

Mindful listening then involves more than hearing with the ears.

From the textbooks of kindergarten teachers and speech language pathologists[@truesdale1990whole]. We will identify its components as:

  • Look at the person who is speaking to you
  • Listen with both of your ears
  • Turn your body towards the other person
  • Keep your lips and extremities still while the other person is speaking
  • Thinking about what they are saying
  • Care about what they are saying

And, for adults: * Keep your devices out of reach

When we speak, we want our listeners to be tuned-in, we want them to connect with us, to correctly understand our problem, or learn about our proposed solutions or insights. It is the rare individual who accomplishes this without actively listening.

Consider for a moment what happens when there is no listening between client and consulting team or within a consulting team. Even the most fundamental of tasks – determining who exactly is on the team – is doomed if there is no listening.

Training Goals Related to Listening

Perhaps the discipline most attuned to the need for listening training is medicine.

Doctors are diagnosticians and healers, AND they possess critical information needed by patients to make vital decisions. While communications training can be found in many medical school programs, it is frequently an elective and sometimes offered only occasionally. Exemplary programs exist and we have adapted some of their strategies, specifically the model presented by the Massachusetts Medical Schools’ Collaborative[@reidy2022paving]

Five competencies are presented that a medical student should possess, we adapt this structure for our consultants:

  1. Consultants should start by assessing the client’s understanding of their own problem and determining their priorities in order to develop an analysis plan.

  2. Consultants should develop their skill of responding in such a way as to develop connection with the client

  3. Consultants should observe and practice conversations related to restating the client’s problem and propose solutions. First in a low stakes learning environment (Stats 140XP) then later on, supervised (Stats 141XP) with moderated real-world clients

  4. Consultants should be equipped with skills assessments and reflective practice essential for developing effective communication

  5. Consultants should be able to field questions and explain the principles and prac- tice of statistics to clients when presenting deliverables The competencies will be addressed as we turn to building listening skills.

Building Listening Skills

Listening is multidimensional and involves both our eyes and ears. Consider the fol- lowing aspects of a speaker’s delivery from a listener’s perspective:

  • speed
  • social context (e.g., asking someone to repeat in a private conversation vs a large auditorium)
  • general vocabulary
  • non-verbal cues
  • context/content of the discussion
  • potential lack of a common language (e.g., non-native speakers but also math or specialized vocabulary)
  • listeners deficits (e.g., lack of sleep, illness, emotions)

Minimizing Distractions, Maximizing Attention

A core support of active listening is attending. In a consulting environment, the con- sultant cannot be become bored or find the client dull as boredom has the power to activate the pain receptors in our brains[@nederkoorn2016self]. In response, we become distracted and lose focus. Consider the list above – anything that might distract us, for example: missing a word from a rapid-fire speaker, not having a good grasp of the content – will draw us away from understanding a client’s needs.

We training our consulting teams to prepare for meetings by encouraging

  1. turning off electronic devices
  2. making the conscious choice to find the speaker interesting
  3. focusing on the words of the client, not the client’s personal appearance
  4. take occasional notes or if the speaker allows it, record the meeting for review
  5. use one’s thinking speed, which is far more rapid than human speech[@korba1990rate] to anticipate the client’s points or to evaluate what has been presented.

The Vocabulary of the Client

Eudaimonia - or what we can control

Epictetus the stoic taught that the road to happiness could be found in these two things:

  1. Identifying (correctly) what is within our control, and what is not.
  2. Investing our energy on the things within our control and learning to cede that which is not.

We can prepare our consultants for our end of the problem (e.g., getting enough sleep before an important meeting) but recognizing that we cannot control every situa- tion. Acknowledgement of the unexpected goes a long way towards successful consulting by helping the consultant maintain composure in every situation.

Overlap with other constructs

Statistical consulting teams share group fundamentals with other disciplines. * A need for well defined roles and responsibilities * A purpose and an end * A well defined structure (e.g., tasks, agreed upon code of conduct) * Guidance from managers, directors, and clients Communication and empathy are strongly tied with listening. Listening is just as vital (if not more) than speaking. Active listening helps us to understand what is being said and can help prevent miscommunication.

The cross-cultural/social issues.

Research on the premature departure of international workers from corporate environ- ments is revealing[@jassawalla2004cross]. There is difficulty in establishing trust or developing relationships. Inability to clearly communicate difficulties or to seek assistance in a foreign environ- ment can lead to early departures.

EXERCISES

Individual exercises

Emotion Review.

This can be practiced when alone and in a calm state of mind.

In a quiet place, think about a recent meaningful (not necessarily work related) conversation. Recall any events that caused an emotional reaction from you.

Now try to think of ways in which you could have handled the situation differently. Example:

Incident: Your friend responds to you in anger because you forgot about a lunch date.

Emotion triggered: Fear. You felt ashamed and had nothing to say so you chose not to respond.

Possible alternative response: Remorse. Express remorse with a sincere apology.

Lessons: To create a sense of empathy, required for mindful listening and to develop emotional intelligence by learning to generate additional possibilities in any situation.

Explore the “SOLER” system

The acronym “SOLER” can help you develop your active listening skills.

  • Sit squarely. Move your body so you face the speaker.
  • Open posture. Whether you’re sitting or standing, keep your hands open and your arms at your sides. Avoid tightening your body or folding your arms and legs when listening. Those body language signals defensiveness, frustration or impatience.
  • Lean toward the speaker. This posture demonstrates you are interested in what the speaker is saying.
  • Eye contact. Maintain comfortable eye contact. A good rule of thumb is to maintain eye contact for about five to 10 seconds, gaze away and look back again.
  • Relax . The more relaxed you seem the more comfortable the speaker feels.

Group Exercises.

Drawing Questions

Have the participants pair up and seat themselves back to back. Hand one member of each pair a drawing (simple) do not show it to the other member. The participant without the drawing is given a blank sheet and something to draw with (pencil, pen, etc.). The “artist” needs to ask questions about the drawing and then draw what they are hearing from their partner. At the end of the activity both are compared to see how accurately the drawing has been replicated.

Lessons

  • The value of clarifying questions and also of framing and asking the right questions.

Ask ‘n’ Tell

Divide your participants into groups of size six or more. Ask a volunteer to share a short story related to a major turning point in their life] So for example, when they had to call “911”. Divide the remainder of the group into “askers” and “tellers.” The Askers” are to ONLY ask questions of the volunteer speaker and the “Tellers” are to tell the group about their comparable experience. Ask the group to find another volunteer and ask the listeners to switch their roles (askers should try telling, tellers should try asking).

Lessons

  • Ask the volunteers when they felt heard – when the “Askers” asked or when the “Tellers” told? Ask the group members to discuss some of their feelings around the responses.

For reflection and discussion - individual and/or group

  1. Recall your interactions with people who are/were “good listeners”, what did you find most engaging or memorable about their conversation(s) with you?

  2. Suppose you have been introduced to a new co-worker consider/reflect upon some aspects mindful listening that might be a challenge for the typical person on first meeting.

  3. Can you recall an example, e.g., fictitious, a personal experience or even a historical one, where better listening was important. For example, a mid-air accident was caused by an air controller who thought he heard the word “passed” instead of the word “passing” and misdirected a plane with fatal consequences.

  4. What suggestions can you offer to someone who wants to improve active listening abilities?

  5. Is there a relationship between active listening and conflict resolution? If yes, how do listening skills relate to conflict resolution? If no, can you provide an example?
