1.1 Elaboration and example of ASA Ethical Guidelines for Individuals and Organizations Engaged in the Practice of Statistics Prepared by the Committee on Professional Ethics of the American Statistical Association Approved by ASA Board of Directors February 1, 2022

Purpose of the Guidelines

The American Statistical Association’s Ethical Guidelines for Statistical Practice are intended to help statistical practitioners make decisions ethically. In these guidelines, “statistical practice” includes activities such as …

  1. Data Collection,

  2. Data Summarization

  3. Data Processing,

  4. Statistical Analysis,

  5. Interpretation, and

  6. Model development.

5 PRINCIPLE A: Professional Integrity and Accountability

Professional integrity and accountability require taking responsibility for one’s work. Ethical statistical practice supports valid and prudent decision-making with appropriate methodology. The ethical statistical practitioner represents their capabilities and activities honestly and treats others with respect.

The ethical statistical practitioner:

1.Takes responsibility for evaluating potential tasks, assessing whether they have (or can attain) sufficient competence to execute each task and that the work and timeline are feasible. Does not solicit or deliver work for which they are not qualified or that they would not be willing to have peer reviewed.

NoteExample

Suppose that a department of statistics and data science offers a consulting course in which students act as statistical consultants and undertake projects that are brought in by different clients. If the instructor is not certain that the team assigned to the project does not have the competence or training to execute the relevant task within the required time frame, this project should not be accepted. This becomes even more problematic if the client intends to publish the results of the project is a peer-reviewed journal. However, if the instructor makes it clear from the beginning that the students’ work will be exploratory, and they will not necessarily be able to answer the question of interest, and the client accepts that, one could go forward with that type of a project.

In many consulting projects, if the consultants believe that they do not have the training to execute the project, they could always seek the advice of an external expert and compensate them accordingly.

2. Uses methodology and data that are valid, relevant, and appropriate, without favoritism or prejudice, and in a manner intended to produce valid, interpretable, and reproducible results.

NoteExample of validity

The purpose of a study was to find out the attitude of Muslim women residing in southern California toward doing mammography as a means of breast cancer prevention as a function of their knowledge of breast cancer. In order to develop a valid survey for the participants’ knowledge of breast cancer, content validity needed to be ascertained by having a few breast cancer specialists examine the survey and make sure the questions asked reflect a valid knowledge of breast cancer.

Example of interpretable results

One of the members on the board wanted to explain the results of the breast cancer study on mass media so that it could be followed by non-statisticians. Logistic regression was used to examine the likelihood of doing a mammography as a function of multiple factors such as knowledge of breast cancer, cultural background, level of education etc. Simple odds ratios within context with no mention of the technicalities were used to explain the findings to the board member.

Example of lack of prejudice

In order to learn more about the beliefs of the Muslim women residing in southern California toward pre-screening for breast cancer, one-on-one interviews were done. The results were so different depending on the country of origin and spoken language. Some women said I must have done something bad and now I am being punished. Others said I do not want anybody to know because my friends will leave me. Others would say, I want to let everyone know and this is something random that happened to me. These one-on-one interviews helped us in understanding and appreciating the differences in attitudes of women from different backgrounds toward pre-screening for breast cancer and how they felt after they had the cancer . These experiences are very helpful in developing lack of prejudice and helping in future projects that a consultant could encounter.

Example of reproducible results

Reproducible results are a lot more possible in conditions where the factors affecting the outcome can be controlled. For example, if one wanted to examine the effect of light, a fertilizer, and water on the growth of tomatoes, as long as one chooses plots of land that were similar in terms of light and irrigation and controlled the concentration of the fertilizer, then the results are much more likely to be reproducible. However, interventions that involve multiple confounding factors that are hard to control, are less likely to produce similar results.

However, one can always make an effort to minimize the effect of confounders. Examples include:

  • Random assignment of participants to control and experimental groups. Random assignment is assumed to establish equivalency between the treatment and control group.

  • Making equivalent groups based on some confounding factor. For instance, if weight or BMI is a confounding factor in a medical intervention, one could rank the participants from the lowest to the height BMI, then make pairs, and assign one member of each pair to the control and the other to the experimental group.

  • Using confounding factors such as prior knowledge as covariates in educational interventions, and

  • Using background variables such as gender, age, ethnicity, and comorbidities such as hypertension, heart issues, diabetic, etc. are used as covariates in medical research.

  • Using a combination of the above; namely random assignment plus covariates is another solution

3. Does not knowingly conduct statistical practices that exploit vulnerable populations or create or perpetuate unfair outcomes.

The tragic tale of the Tuskegee Syphilis Study serves as an illustration1. From 1932 to 1972, the U.S. Public Health Service conducted a study on the progression of untreated syphilis in African American men in Macon County, Alabama. The participants were not informed of their diagnosis, not given informed consent, and were not provided with effective treatment even after penicillin was recognized as the standard of care for syphilis. The statistical data gathered from this unethical experiment was used in medical literature for many years, highlighting a significant abuse of trust and misuse of research ethics.

4. Opposes efforts to predetermine or influence the results of statistical practices and resists pressure to selectively interpret data.

Lack of understanding between practical and statistical significance is frequently a source of pressure from the client with respect to interpretation of the findings.

Example. No matter what statistical method is used, if the sample size is large enough, the standard error will decrease resulting in high t or F values and p-values less than 0.05, indicating statistical significance. Whereas, with large enough sample sizes, p-values less than 0.05 are highly likely to happen with low effect size, example lack of practical difference between the mean of the experimental and control group along with low power, or negligible R-squared in regression analysis, or very low accuracy in machine learning models such as logistic or ordinal regression.

Therefore, in situations with very large sample sizes leading to statistical but no practical significance, the statistical consultant needs to explain to the client that p-value smaller than 0.05 are necessary but not sufficient for reporting major findings and potentially asking for further funding.

5. Accepts full responsibility for their own work, does not take credit for the work of others, and gives credit to those who contribute. Respects and acknowledges the intellectual property of others.

Statistical consultation is not a solo process and always involves collaboration between a team of people. This was illustrated in the chapters that explain the multiple dimensions involved in successful team work including active listening, communication, having an internal locus of control, conflict resolution, empathy and team building. Thus, when a statistical consultant is asked to take the major role in designing a study, advise on development of instruments, data collection, analysis, and reporting of the data, the consultant should have respect for the work of all members and endorse them as needed.

6. Strives to follow, and encourages all collaborators to follow, an established protocol for authorship. Advocates for recognition commensurate with each person’s contribution to the work. Recognizes that inclusion as an author does imply, while acknowledgement may imply, endorsement of the work.

When it comes to publishing the work in refereed journal articles, the statistician along with the consultant need to:

  1. ascertain that the paper is written along with the guideline and standard of the journal of interest

  2. decide on the order of authorship based on the contributions of each team member.

What has to be pointed out is that the level of teaching and leadership that the statistical consultant is responsible for varies with the project. If the consultant is collaborating with researchers who are well known in the field, have an established theory, and want to find out the extent to which the data collected by them supports the hypotheses they want to prove, the role of the statistical consultant may be limited to advising the client on the right statistical method, overseeing analysis of the data, ascertaining that the relevant assumptions are met, and explaining the results in a language that is clear and understandable to the consumers of that research.

However, in some other projects, the statistical consultant is in the scene from the inception to the completion of the project. He may be asked to answer a question about a topic with which he is totally unfamiliar. He needs to spend some time learning about the topic, and then deciding what measurements he needs to add the relevant data. Then, the role of the statistical consultant would be different and a lot more guidance and leadership is expected.

Example. In a study (Esfandiari), the objective was to build a survey to measure ethics of elementary school children who were being taught how to create a business toward entrepreneurship. The first step was to invite a few major entrepreneurs and talk to them about the definition of “who is an entrepreneur” and what consists of ethical behavior in the field of entrepreneurship. After one-on-one interviews with these individuals and reading the relevant theory, it was decided that surveys would be useless for assessing the attitude of elementary school children toward entrepreneurship. Consequently, it was decided to create a survey that consisted of small case studies to which elementary school children could identify. For example, one item sounded as follows.

Mary sits at the same table with two more students, Jane and Deborah. Mary likes red pens but he does not have any. Jane has five red pens. Would be OK if Mary takes one of Jane’s red pens.

  1. It would be fine because Jane will still have four red pens.

  2. It would be fine if she asks one of the red pens but lets Jane know

  3. It would not be right for her to take one of the red pens.

What was interesting was that after studying the literature, we found that one always needs to include one ethical, one unethical, and one conditionally ethical items because children who are at a certain age may not yet have developed the relevant schemata, menta structure, to have a full understanding and interpretation of abstract concepts such as ethical behavior.

7. Discloses conflicts of interest, financial and otherwise, and manages or resolves them according to established policies, regulations, and laws.

One of the major dimensions of EQ that was discussed in the prior chapter is learning about the constructive methods of “conflict resolution”, which is a field of itself in the world of today. We have experienced this multiple times in working with teams of students on the consultation process. We offer an example:

Example. In one of the projects during the pandemic the Asian students were interested in analyzing the president Trump’s tweets calling covid-19, the “Chinese Virus” and the ill effects that it had on the Asian community. Half of the team members were complaining that others had stolen the project and were running with it. The other half were complaining that they are not following anything and nobody answers their questions and they felt excluded.

To solve this issue, we took the following steps:

  1. Talked to each member alone and listened to what they had to say.

  2. Talked with the whole team and asked them to communicate their concerns with the rest.

  3. This created a much more comfortable atmosphere among the members because they all felt they were being heard.

It looked like half of the students on this team were more advanced and were using methods like NLP (natural language processing), and similar techniques to analyze the tweets. Whereas, the other group members were not familiar with such techniques.

After talking to everyone, they decided to divide the task according to their qualifications and interest and it all worked well. However, the major thing was to use the right leadership, hear everyone, and guide them to come up with a decision that worked for the entire team.

8. Promotes the dignity and fair treatment of all people. Neither engages in nor condones discrimination based on personal characteristics. Respects personal boundaries in interactions and avoids harassment, including sexual harassment, bullying, and other abuses of power or authority.

In the time of post-Covid, one of the projects that the students worked on was a content analysis of Trump’s comments calling it the “Chinese Virus”. One group of Chinese enrolled students decided to take Trump’s tweets on this subject and employ different methodologies such as NLP (Natural Language Processing) and other related methods (Wordcloud, Sentiment Analysis) for finding patterns and development of predictive models of the relevant data. The group consisted of eight members and with two or three weeks to complete the project, half of the members complained that the other half had co-opted the project and they were not being treated as an integral part of the team.

In order to resolve this problem we first spoke to the members individually and tell their story. This needed 15-30 minutes per person, then we brought the entire group together to speak and listen to each other. The conclusion we reached was not one of disrespect or exclusion but instead some were more advanced in their techniques and were too excited about modeling. The others were not well trained enough to follow along and felt excluded.

We came to the conclusion that it would be best to divide the team into two separate groups and have each group work on a project tailored to their own interest. Both groups wound up being quite successful with equally sound results having used different datasets.

The conclusion reached was that in order to promote dignity and inclusion, it is best to invest time in listening to individuals and then to the entire group before making a decision as to what to do.

NoteConflict Resolution Process

  1. Acknowledge the conflict existed

  2. Talk to everyone individually and within the group to clarify

  3. During the group meeting, we listened and while some agreed verbally, we had to look beyond their words to learn the truth of the team dynamics.

  4. We tried to work on an action plan that was acceptable to everyone before the groups reconvened.

  5. We sought feedback and agreed on a plan of action. proposing a solution involving common goals and interests stratified by ability

  6. We closely monitored the progress of each group until they finished their projects.

9. Take appropriate action when aware of deviations from these guidelines by others.

In the consulting course, we require students to give us the following:

  • R Code

  • Codebook

  • Cleaned Data

  • Final Report

All results must be reproducible, therefore all project work must have a clear workflow from raw data to final product. In this way, deviations can be identified quickly therefore it is not in any team’s interest to deviate from agreed guidelines and rules. In many projects, our clients continue to modify the raw data and desire to re-run the new data using the code provided by the teams.

10. Acquires and maintains competence through upgrading of skills as needed to maintain a high standard of practice.

In the consulting course, we ascertain that the students are able to integrate the previously acquired knowledge to solve real problems and also introduce additional more advanced methods to continue building their skills for specific clients.

Example. ROC are widely used in the biological sciences to report the accuracy of medical treatments as well as concepts such as false positive, true negative etc. Once students have demonstrated mastery they are better able to help our clients.

We are frequently the recipients of data from survey organizations within the university and outside. For example, student attitudes on current topics or educational interventions on changes in attitude. In our department, we do not offer courses in scale construction, generalizability theory, evaluation of reliability or factor or cluster analysis. We teach these methods in the consulting course encouraging continuing education and upgrading of skills.

12. Upholds, respects, and promotes these guidelines. Those who teach, train, or mentor in statistical practice have a special obligation to promote behavior that is consistent with these guidelines.

We promote and teach the ethical guidelines of ASA though the following channels

  1. We require the students to read, analyze, and discuss the ethical guidelines of ASA on their own and as a team

  2. As checking for understanding we have teams of students: 1) analyze case studies in which ethical guidelines of ASA have been breached, 2) identify the unethical practices that occurred, and 3) propose a solution for correcting the unethical practice of data science in the context discussed.

  3. As homework we have teams: 1) read, analyze, and summarize the guidelines of the ASA on their own and as a team, 2) write case studies in which some of the Ethical Guidelines of ASA are breached, watch videos of studies in which guidelines of ASA have been breached and answer questions about instances in which unethical practice of data science has happened.

Ethical Guideline II.A.7: The fact that a procedure is automated does not ensure its correctness or appropriateness; it is also necessary to understand the theory, data, and methods used in each statistical study. This goal is served best when a competent statistical practitioner is included early in the research design, preferably in the planning stage.*

Ethical Guideline II.A.8: Recognize that any frequentist statistical test has a random chance of indicating significance when it is not really present. 6 PRINCIPLE B: Integrity of Data and Methods

(Vivian)

Communicates data sources and fitness for use, including data generation and collection processes and known biases. Discloses and manages any conflicts of interest relating to the data sources. Communicates data processing and transformation procedures, including missing data handling.

  1. Is transparent about assumptions made in the execution and interpretation of statistical practices, including methods used, limitations, possible sources of error, and algorithmic biases. Conveys results or applications of statistical practices in ways that are honest and meaningful.

Example. A student team was examining the results of a surgery where a small percentage failed. This is a situation where a contingency is unbalanced and did not meet the assumption of standard logistic regression and instead moved a rare events method (logistf in R). In terms of algorithmic bias, the patient database used for the training of the models is predominant white and male and results may not generalize beyond this population. The student team conveyed their warnings and assumptions clearly to the client.

  1. Communicates the stated purpose and the intended use of statistical practices. Is transparent regarding a priori versus post hoc objectives and planned versus unplanned statistical practices. Discloses when multiple comparisons are conducted and any relevant adjustments.

Example. A medical doctor is conducting a study to test the effectiveness of wearable technology which monitors blood glucose and blood pressure on the reduction of emergency room visits. The study involves two groups of participants: one receiving this device (treatment group) and the other receiving a similar device without the ability to monitor blood glucose and blood pressure (placebo/control group). He has approached our student consultants for assistance.

The consulting team clearly communicates that the primary objective of the statistical analysis is to compare the difference in emergency room visits between the treatment and control groups.

They intend to use poisson regression to determine if the difference in the count of E is statistically significant.

The A priori objective was to compare the count of ER visits. This was established before the study began. The Post hoc objective was developed after observing the data. The team decided to also compare the interaction between the two groups. This is disclosed as a post hoc analysis since it was not part of the original study design.

  1. Meets obligations to share the data used in the statistical practices (e.g., for peer review and replication) as allowable. Respects expectations of data contributors when using or sharing data. Exercises due caution to protect proprietary and confidential data, including all data that might inappropriately harm data subjects.

Example. In a consulting course designed for statistics and data science majors, teams of students work with a real client. The client provides them with the data set, and the questions to be answered. As the first step of dealing with big data, the team is expected to create subsets of data that include the potential variables that are of interest to the client, think of methods that have the least bias for replacement of missing data, and seek the input of the client with regards to cut off points for changing numerical to categorical data.

This happens a lot in medical consultation. For example, age is a contributing factor to hypertension. Often clients want the odds of hypertension as a function of age range. Student teams cannot decide on intervals that look statistically feasible. For example, they cannot divide age by ten year intervals because the odds of hypertension does not change every ten years. It might not change much till sixty and then have a major spike. Rather, the statistical consultant needs to ask the client about the appropriate cutoff points. Additionally, at the termination of the project, the team is supposed to provide the client with a clean data set, a clear codebook explaining how each variable were measured, and computer codes that allow the client to rerun the results after additional data has been added so that the same results could be reproduced.

Additionally, the statistical consultant should ascertain that the data are de-identified, if needed get IRB (Institutional Review Board) approval. If the statisticians are consulting with an institution, they cannot use their data for teaching or publish the results unless they have permission from the relevant resources. Therefore, it is clear that multiple steps need to be taken to keep the confidentiality and integrity of the data provided by the client.

  1. Strives to promptly correct substantive errors discovered after publication or implementation. As appropriate, disseminates the correction publicly and/or to others relying on the results.

Example. Sometimes when the clients want to publish the results of the work done by student consultants in a refereed journal article, they might come back and ask for additional analysis or explanation of the findings. In that case, the results produced by the student teams are re-examined and reanalyzed if so that the potential mistakes made by the student teams are corrected and the manuscript is ready for publication in a refereed journal. In situations like this the help of another statistical consultant or graduate student who is well versed in the methodology can be sought and generally they are offered co-authorship.

  1. For models and algorithms designed to inform or implement decisions repeatedly, develop and/or implement plans to validate assumptions and assess performance over time, as needed. Considers criteria and mitigation plans for model or algorithm failure and retirement.

Example. In an on-going relationship with a client, the research needs and the underlying data can change. Electronic medical records are such an example. Patient data is continually updated with each visit and possible adverse events. Statistical analyses, dashboards, and models need to be robust and/or modifiable to keep up with the changing needs. A dashboard example might be a predictive model of congestive heart failure as a function of factors such as age, weight, gender, presence of diabetes, etc where the client has the ability to update the data to generate new predictions.

  1. Explores and describes the effect of variation in human characteristics and groups on statistical practice when feasible and relevant.

Example. In a study we wanted to examine the effect of two teaching methods, case-based instruction vs. theory on teaching students, who were trained to be math and science teachers at high school level, how to apply theories of motivation and multiculturalism in actual teaching. The student teachers were randomly assigned to two groups. One group was taught theories of motivation and multiculturalism plus real case studies in which the teachers showed how to apply these principles in everyday teaching.

At the end of the study it was shown that strong students (top 25%) did equally well whether they were taught by theory or case-based instruction. While students that had challenges (bottom 25%), did a lot better with case-based instruction. It is like analyses of cases helped them decipher, and analyze the theoretical principles behind motivation and multiculturalism while learning how to apply it in reality.

In a similar study we showed that case-based instruction helps to close the gap between the performance of statistics and social science majors in applied statistics class.

7 PRINCIPLE C: Responsibilities to Stakeholders (Mahtash)

  1. Seeks to establish what stakeholders hope to obtain from any specific project. Strives to obtain sufficient subject-matter knowledge to conduct meaningful and relevant statistical practice.

(NEW)Striving to obtain sufficient subject-matter knowledge to conduct meaningful and relevant statistical practice.

Example one. In the second part of the consulting course, the student teams are assigned to work with real clients from different disciplines including medicine, administration, education, and public health, etc.. Generally speaking the statistics and data science majors are not familiar with subject matter knowledge in medical topics such as diabetes, liver transplantation, eye surgery, and relevant comorbidities.

One of the major messages that we try to give the student teams is that in the majority of consulting projects, the statistical consultant is not familiar with the subject-matter knowledge of the project. Through the consulting project and working with the client the teams learn that they can acquire the relevant subject-matter knowledge by reading the background literature recommended by the client, ascertaining that they have a clear codebook explaining the relevant variables and how they were measured (interval, nominal, ordinal, etc.), making sure which variables the clients want to include in the study, and a good understanding of relevant comorbidities (the simultaneous presence of two or more diseases or medical conditions in a patient) and covariates (variables that are correlated with the outcome and could potentially affect the results). Through reading the background literature, learning about the variables of the study, and communication with the client regarding potential covariates and comorbidities, the teams can make better decisions regarding the application of relevant and meaningful statistical practice.

For example, in the evaluation of an educational intervention on the effect of an after-school program on standardized math scores for middle school students from lower socio-economic-status, the team found a very high correlation between pretest and posttest standardized score on math. When they used the pretest scores as a predictor, they found a very high R-squared.   1. problem with repetition of the same statistical method

One of the situations in which ethical practice of statistics can be breached, is frequent use of the same statistical technique. For example, if we wanted to examine the effect of an intervention on multiple outcomes, repeated use of the two-sample test of the mean would increase type I error. We are assuming that all these repeated t-tests are independent of each other. For instance, if we want to examine the effect of an intervention (like a teaching method/or medication) on five different numerical outcomes that could potentially be correlated, the five repeated use of two-sample test of the mean will decrease the confidence level to (0.95)^5 = 0.66; assuming alpha of 0.05. Consequently, the confidence level will decrease to 0.66 and the p-level or the actual risk of rejecting a true null will increase to 0.34. In such a case use of a one factor MANOVA (multivariate analysis of variance) is recommended.

The same dilemma could happen with multiple use of Chi-square to examine the relationship b multiple categorical predictors. This problem would be solved with the use of classification methods such as logistic or multinomial regression, depending on whether the outcome is binary or has more than one level.

Suppose you want to predict the relationship between standardized test scores with high school and college GPA. Repeated use of simple linear regression is another error. This is due to the fact that the predictors are highly likely correlated to each other and we are presented with a problem of multicollinearity so that the sum of R-squared resulting from the sum of two simple linear regression is not equal to the R-squared resulting from multiple linear regression.

  1. Applying the same statistical method to problems of different nature

One can come across situations where the same statistical method such as multiple linear regression is applied to data that are non-linear without making the right transformations. This creates the major problem of meeting the assumption of linearity and therefore, the results not valid and reliable.

Suppose we use a linear regression model to predict test scores from the instructional method. While presenting this model to clients, it is important to inform them that this model is based on this specific data set and assumes a linear relationship. This model may not be accurate for a different school or set of students or if the relationship between the method and the test scores is non-linear. We would also suggest alternative solutions if the stakeholder can foresee changes in the design and collection of data.

  1. Sample Size Determination: Sample size involves a balance between having a large enough sample to detect an effect (power), but not excessively large which wastes resources or unnecessarily exposes too many subjects to possible risk.

Example. The best example here is the Gallup poll. When Gallup wants to predict the future elections of politicians, they suffice with samples of 2000 and not higher. The statistical method that relates to choosing or not choosing a political candidate is one sample proportion assuming that the proportion of the population who vote for candidate X is 50% or 0.50. We can use the following formula to estimate the sample size

\(`N\ = \ \frac{Z^{2}*(pq)}{(ME)^{2}}`\)

  • 95% confidence interval for a two sided test, then Z would be 1.96

  • p and q equal to 0.50

  • ME (margin of error) is equal to 0.02

Then N would be equal to almost 2400

\(`N\ = \ \frac{{1.96}^{2}*(0.25)}{(0.0004)}`\) = 2401

So, with a margin of error of 2%, we need a sample size of about 2400. Now if we increase the margin of error to 1%, the sample size needed would be

\(`N\ = \ \frac{{1.96}^{2}*(0.25)}{(0.0001)^{}}`\) = 9604

When we cut the margin of error in half the sample becomes four times and therefore, the cost increases a lot. Given the margin of error of 2% is acceptable and provides enough power, it is not necessary to go to 1%.

The same is true for clinical trials involving analyses such as two sample test of the mean; comparing two groups or one-way anova comparing multiple groups. In the beginning phases one can start with a smaller sample and in order to reach the power of interest and a reasonable effect size, the sample can be increased.

  1. Statistical Methodology: Ethical considerations might also affect the choice of statistical methods. For example, if the study involves multiple comparisons, appropriate statistical techniques should be used to control the risk of Type I error (false positives). Failing to do so could lead to incorrect conclusions and potentially harmful consequences if interventions are incorrectly deemed effective. Similarly, using statistical methodologies that handle missing data appropriately is crucial, as poor handling can lead to biased results.

Example One. Suppose that in a study we are comparing the effect of three doses of medication on lowering blood pressure. The outcome variable being drop in blood pressure after a period of two months, and the independent variable being the treatment or the three doses of medication.

The null hypothesis tested here is that the three doses of medicine are equally effective in lowering blood pressure. The methodology to use would be one-way anova and if the F-test were significant, we would conclude that the three doses of medicine differ in their effective and we need to follow the analyses with post-hocs like Tukey HSD, which would make all possible pairwise comparisons (method one vs two, one vs three and two vs three) while keeping the error rate at 5%. The most effective way of reporting the findings to the client would be a plot of the means along with error bars reflecting the mean and standard deviation of each group.

Now suppose that a statistical consultant decides to repeat the two sample test of the mean three times comparing group one with two, one with three, and two with three. He is now making the assumption that these comparisons are independent of each other; which is wrong. Assuming that alpha level is 0.05 for each two-sample test of the mean, if they were independent, the overall error would be (0.05)^3 or 0.15 thus reducing the confidence level to 0.85. This is an example of using the wrong statistical method for comparison of the effectiveness of interventions.

Example two. Suppose that you wanted to examine the effect of three GRE preparation programs on being admitted to a school that ranks in the top 10%. So, in this case the outcome variable is binary which is being admitted vs not being admitted to a school in the top 10%. The predictor is categorical with three levels. The right method would be using logistic regression to compare the odds of being accepted to a school in the top 10% for the three programs. The wrong method would be repeating Chi-square analysis three times. Something similar to what was explained in example one.

2. A comprehensive Exploratory Data Analysis

A complete EDA (Exploratory data analysis) which involves histogram, frequency tables, contingency tables, side-by-side box plots, correlation matrices, etc. (Esfandiari) Experience has shown that EDA is the most important step in a project, It is during this step that we discover if histograms are skewed and need transformation, this is when we come across situation that none of the transformations used help to create normality in the numerical variables. The next thing we might think of is cutting the numerical variable into categories.

As statisticians we tend to create the cuts that look best from the statistical point of view such as cutting at the median or every quartile to create similar frequencies in the different categories. While after talking to the client, you are instructed that the cut needs to be made at a specific point. This happens very often in medical research where the client would tell you the cut needs to be at a specific number like 4.1 millimeter or a blood pressure of 180/90. This has been very educational for the teams because they learn the importance of communication and involving the client when they are making transformation to the variables.

Another important thing that has happened very often is creation of contingency tables to show the relationship between categorical variables. In many circumstances, the frequency tables generated do not have similar or close numbers in the different cells. Therefore, the application of standard machine learning methods such as logistic for multinomial regression leads to range results in P-value. This is a result of very low cell frequency and the assumptions of the model are not met. In such circumstances one should either employ methodologies designed for rare events or utilize non-parametric statistical methods.

Non-parametric statistical methods are not part of our undergraduate curriculum. However, since they frequently arise in studies where data is hard to collect, we discuss them. For example, experience has shown us that sometimes three different hospitals must collaborate to create a data set that shows a rare surgery for 40 patients. These experiences have taught the teams the importance of EDA and data cleaning. Based on the talk by one of our prior students with five years of experience in the industry, he mentioned that I spent 60% of my time on data cleaning and doing EDA.

These experiences have taught our students that the worst practice for a data scientist would be to try to match a statistical method to the problem presented and start running the model and creating the outcome. What happens is that they end up creating lengthy outputs which they do not know how to interpret.

For example, on the research on areas like campus climate and diversity when I have asked the students about their ethnic background, and suppose we get ten different answers. Another question is whether our campus climate is very comfortable, comfortable, somewhat comfortable, uncomfortable, or very uncomfortable. Suppose the research question of interest is:

Does the effect of comfort in our climate on the sense of belonging to our campus (numerical) vary with ethnicity?

The answer requires us to study the interaction between ethnicity and comfort level. This would create a ten by five contingency table with varying frequencies and sparsity in some cells. Once the regression model is run, it will create many meaningless interaction effects with strange p-values. Then the challenge is: What does this weird model mean and how do I interpret it?

However, EDA would help to pool together certain categories on campus climate and ethnicity and a contingency table with reasonable frequencies and then the created model would make sense, the coefficients would have higher reliability/consistency, and the data would be interpretable. Exposure to such practices helps the teams experience first hand the importance of the reasonable number of observations on the reliability, and interpretability of the coefficients of the model as well as its reproducibility.

12 PRINCIPLE H: Responsibilities Regarding Potential Misconduct(BOTH)

  1. Knows the definitions of and procedures relating to misconduct in their institutional setting. Seeks to clarify facts and intent before alleging misconduct by others. Recognizes that differences of opinion and honest error do not constitute unethical behavior.

  2. Avoids condoning or appearing to condone statistical, scientific, or professional misconduct. Encourages other practitioners to avoid misconduct or the appearance of misconduct.

  3. Does not make allegations that are poorly founded or intended to intimidate. Recognizes such allegations as potential ethics violations.

  4. Lodges complaints of misconduct discreetly and to the relevant institutional body.

Does not act on allegations of misconduct without appropriate institutional referral, including those allegations originating from social media accounts or email listservs.

  1. Insists upon a transparent and fair process to adjudicate claims of misconduct. Maintains confidentiality when participating in an investigation. Discloses the investigation results honestly to appropriate parties and stakeholders once they are available.

  2. Refuses to publicly question or discredit the reputation of a person based on a specific accusation of misconduct while due process continues to unfold.

  3. Following an investigation of misconduct, supports the efforts of all parties involved to resume their careers in as normal a manner as possible, consistent with the outcome of the investigation.

  4. Avoids and acts to discourage retaliation against or damage to the employability of those who responsibly call attention to possible misconduct.
