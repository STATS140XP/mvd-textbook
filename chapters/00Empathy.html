Empathy

“When you start to develop your powers of empathy and imagination, the whole world opens up to you.”
— Susan Sarandon

Empathy arises from authentic concern for others. It refers to the capacity to read and understand the emotions and, to a degree, the dispositions of others; the positive ability to vicariously understand the circumstances of others, including teammates, clients, and stakeholders — to stand in someone else’s shoes, to view the world through the eyes of another.

Empathy is very closely related to Emotional Intelligence, or EQ, or sometimes, EI. EQ is usually regarded is a broader skill set that includes empathy, but also includes one’s ability to monitor and regulate their own emotions, and also often includes fluency understanding social dynamics.

Empathy includes two aspects or components, cognitive empathy and affective, or emotional empathy.

Cognitive empathy refers to the ability to read social cues, and vicariousness, i.e., the ability to imagine the circumstances of others. Affective empathy refers to the ability to feel and understand the emotions of others. People high in cognitive empathy, but low in affective empathy, may attempt to manipulate others. Extreme cases of low affective empathy is associated with sociopathy. It is traditionally held that the opposite case, being high in affective empathy and low in cognitive empathy is associated with spectrum disorders, like Asperger Syndrome, although some recent research may be challenging this tradition. When we speak of being high in empathy, we mean being high in both aspects.

Empathy is usually thought of as a disposition, or trait, meaning that it is deep seated within the individual. However, both aspects can be learned and developed, especially over time.

Empathy & The Successful Statistical Consultant

The importance of Empathy and EQ have been extensively studied in organizational, leadership, and psychological literature. It is almost impossible to overstate its role in individual and group success.

Like other qualities discussed in this chapter, in particular, flexibility, empathy is important for the statistical consultant both in terms of developing strong interpersonal relationships with teammates, stakeholders, and clients, and also empowering effective problem-solving.

Team members possessing empathy are able to connect with the ideas of others. Teams comprised of empathetic members have discernment; the ability to distinguish and weight the ideas of others. They also work with a larger constellation of alternatives; they are able to understand and weight the positive aspects of the ideas of others. This combinations of discernment and the ability to envision a large space of possible solutions makes empathic team highly constructive. Even during times of challenge, such as changing objectives and tight deadlines, empathic teams are able to find a way forward.

Exercises

Small Group

This will involve upping the intimacy of the initial icebreaker and we would suggest that you prepare the participants in advance (e.g., we’re going to ask you to talk about yourself).

Play it again - Empathy, Flexibility, Communication, Active listening

Purpose: To help participants move outside of their comfort zone in a safe environment and to learn something interesting/memorable about the others.

Divide the participants into pairs, ask them to introduce themselves to the other person by talking about their life trajectory to this point.

.

Then, apologize or smile wickedly... and ask the participants to reintroduce themselves and talk about their life trajectory in a different way.

Here is my example, "Hi, I’m Vivian Lew and I am a lecturer in Statistics at UCLA and the assistant director of the Statistical Consulting Center. I attended UCLA as an undergraduate and took all of my degrees (Bachelor’s, MS, PhD) here. My first real job was as a GIS consultant, then I accepted a position with the "program in statistics" and I stayed with it.

"Hi, I’m Vivian Lew, I was born and raised in Los Angeles to a Chinese immigrant family and being the eldest ABC (American Born Chinese) in my generation, it was challenging because I was fully expected to complete my family’s dreams and encourage those younger than me to do the same. My father failed out of his undergraduate but his life experience served as the foundation of my motivation to pursue a PhD. That decision led me to a life in academia."

Fear/Frustration post its or cards

(needs a moderator and advance planning)

Purpose: to build trust/empathy by having one’s fear anonymously but openly acknowledged, without being subject to ridicule. This activity also helps unity, as people come to realize that everyone has similar fears.

Ideally, this could be played by the entire group of participants using a moderator. We recommend a group no smaller than size 12 in order to protect the privacy/anonymity of the individual.

  1. Ask everyone to complete this sentence on an index card

    (anonymously): "I worry about..." Tell them to be as specific and as honest as possible, but not in such a way that they could be easily identified. Provide an example, such as, "After a couple weeks managing a new class I am afraid that I am not connecting with my students." After everyone is done writing a fear/worry, collect each index card into a large hat/bag/box.

  2. Explain that each person will have a turn to pick an index card. He/she will read the concern aloud and attempt to explain what the person who wrote the worry means, trying to describe his/her understanding/reflection of the person’s emotions associated with the worry.

  3. Explain that there will be no comments on what the reader said.

    1. Participants should simply listen and go on to the next reader.

    2. All participants should respect one another’s privacy and feelings while they are reading their worries.

  4. After all fears have been read and elaborated, discuss as a whole group what some of the common fears were.
